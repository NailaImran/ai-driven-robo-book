# Glossary - Chapter 1

## Physical AI
The integration of artificial intelligence with physical systems, enabling machines to perceive, reason about, and interact with the physical world through sensors and actuators.

## Embodied Intelligence
Intelligence that emerges from the interaction between an agent's body, brain, and environment, emphasizing the role of physical embodiment in cognition.

## Actuator
A mechanical device that converts energy (electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic) into motion, enabling robots to move and interact with their environment.

## Sensor
A device that detects and measures physical properties (light, temperature, pressure, etc.) and converts them into electrical signals for processing.

## CapEx (Capital Expenditure)
Upfront costs for purchasing hardware and equipment that provide long-term value.

## OpEx (Operating Expenditure)
Recurring monthly or annual costs for services, cloud computing, electricity, and maintenance.

## Servo Motor
A rotary actuator that allows for precise control of angular position, velocity, and acceleration.

## IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)
A sensor that measures specific force, angular rate, and sometimes magnetic field, used for motion tracking and orientation.

## Edge Computing
Computing that occurs at or near the source of data generation (on the device) rather than in a centralized cloud data center.

## Degrees of Freedom (DOF)
The number of independent ways a robotic system can move, typically referring to joints or axes of motion.

## GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)
A specialized processor designed for parallel processing, widely used for AI model training and inference.

## Latency
The delay between an input action and the system's response, critical for real-time robotics applications.

## ROS (Robot Operating System)
An open-source middleware framework for robot software development, providing tools and libraries for building robot applications.

## TPU (Tensor Processing Unit)
Google's custom-developed AI accelerator chip optimized for machine learning workloads.

## Computer Vision
A field of AI that enables computers to derive meaningful information from digital images and videos.
